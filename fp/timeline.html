jan

january:

flor de jamaica

flor de jamaica, also known as hibiscus, is usually harvested around the months of december and january. it’s most commonly used for “agua de jamaica” which is a sweetened hibiscus tea, served cold. jamaica releases a red pigment, making agua de jamaica a rich dark red color.

agua de jamaica can be found at most food places as a staple drink alongside tamarind water and horchata. people also commonly make it at home by adding dried jamaica and sugar to a pot of boiling water.

jamaica has become such a prevalent element in mexican culture, that one of the largest flower markets in mexico city is called “el mercado de jamaica”.

feb

february:

bugambilia

bugambilia is a flower that blooms all year. it can be found in all regions of the country. their bright pink color has become emblematic of mexico, and closely resembles a color called “rosa mexicano” which translates to “mexican pink”.

frida kahlo, one of mexico’s most famous painters, was often photographed wearing bugambilias on her head. honoring traditinal mexican hairstyles by incorporating ribbons and flowers into her braided updos, frida’s use of bugambilia is a nod to mexican culture.

mar

march:

jacaranda

jacaranda trees begin to bloom in early spring and can be found all throughout mexico city. this flower is known for its bright lilac color that transforms the streets at its peak. they are associated with the beginning of the springtime.

as jacarandas cover the city in purple, so does one of the biggest events in mexico city. march 8th, known as international women’s day, is when people will wear purple to to the women’s march in mexico.

this movement calls for women’s rights, gender equity, and reparative justice, as mexico continues to be one of the countries with the highest rates of femicide and gendered violence.

apr

april:

velo de novia

velo de novia, which translates directly to “bride’s veil,” is a small wispy white flower that blooms all year. it is often used as a final addition to bouquets, making them look more lively. they are also known as nubes, which translate to “clouds.

velo de novia is a staple at most flower stands in mexico. these can be found on most streets, usually in the form of a kiosk.

the culture around flowers is so prominent that there are various markets and festivals specifically dedicated to the appreciation and sale of flowers.

may

may:

alcatraz

alcatraz flowers are bright white with a yellow center, and usually start blooming at the beginning of the summer. this flower is associated with celebration and can be commonly seen at weddings.

“the flower seller” is just one of many paintings by diego rivera that depicted the culture around flower selling in mexico. these paintings were meant to highlight mexican culture, leading to alcatraz being seen as a uniquely mexican flower.

the women depicted selling these flowers are often indigenous, thus referincing back to mexico’s historical heritage and culture.

jun

june:

flor de nopal

flor de nopal is the flower that grows on top of cactus. it usually blooms in the summer, along with the production of the prickly pear fruit. the flower is usually a bright pink or yellow color, and sits at the top of the cactus, forming a crown-like pattern.

cactus is a staple in mexican cuisine, and can be incorporated in both sweet ans savory dishes.

the cactus itself is often grilled and can be eaten plain with salt or as a side dish.

the prickly pear plant can be eaten as a fruit, but due to the seeds, it’s often blended and served as a drink instead.

jul

july:

flor de calabaza

flor de calabaza is the flower that blooms from the zucchini plant (called "calabaza" in spanish). this is a vibrant orange and yellow flower with thick petals. in mexico, flor de calabaza can often be seen as a healthy food.

the most popular way to eat flor de calabaza is in a quesadilla. quesadillas in mexico are a popular breakfast food, that can also be eaten as a snack. fun fact: in mexico city, quesadillas don’t come with cheese unless you ask for it.

aug

august:

dahlia

dahlias bloom around the summer, in a wide variety of colors. they are also the official national flower of mexico. the aztecs knew them as acocoxochitl, which means “hollow stems with water”. dahlias’ geometric petal pattern make them a good ornamental flower for events.

dahlias are also used in food and medicine. one of its uses is in a traditional drink called atole, which is a thick mix of water, corn flour, and sugar. atole can be consumed year-round and is seen as a traditional comforting drink.

sep

september:

flor amarilla

flor amarilla in spanish means “yellow flower”. because of their vibrant and cheerful color, yellow flowers can often be seen incorporated into bouquets for celebrations. there are many types of flowers that can be yellow including tulips, sunflowers, marigolds, buttercups, etc.

in 2004, a show called “floricienta” became popular across latin america. one of the hit songs, “flores amarillas” became so popular, that people started gifting their friends and family yellow flowers on september 21st to show their appreciation for them.

oct

october:

mano de leon

mano de leon is a popular flower that is most commonly seen in the months of october and november. its name translates to “lion’s paw” because of its uniquely soft and velvety texture. people also call it terciopelo (meaning “felt”), cresta de gallo (meaning “rooster’s crown”), and pata de elefante (meaning “elephant’s foot”).

mano de leon’s most popular use is for day of the dead altars. because of its vibrant purplish pinkish color, it is used to decorate and bring a sense of liveliness and celebration. color is a staple in mexican aesthetics, and this is often reflected its traditional decor and flowers.

nov

november:

cempasuchil

cempasuchil is a bright orange flower that is emblematic of mexico’s day of the dead. this flower maintains most of most of its nahuatl name, cempohualxochitl, which means twenty flowers. it’s named this because cempasuchil naturally blooms in groups of 20 flowers at a time.

dia de los muertos is celebrated on november 1st and 2nd. on these days, people will put up ofrendas (altars) for their loved ones that have passed, with pictures of them, their favorite foods and objects, and cempasuchil flowers.

it is said that on this day, people can be reunited with their loved ones, as they visit them from the other world. this was originally an indigenous tradition that has continued into modern mexico.

dec

december:

nochebuena

nochebuenas, also known as poinsettias, are bright red flowers that typically bloom at the end of the year. nochebuena directly translates to “good night” but is also in reference to the night before christmas (dec. 24th), also called nochebuena.

beyond putting out nochebuenas, holiday festivities include throwing posadas, which are holiday parties. at these posadas, people will break piñatas, sing seasonal songs, and drink ponche, a hot cider-like drink made of apple, guava, hawthorn, tamarind, and cane sugar. ponche will sometimes include jamaica as well (see january).
